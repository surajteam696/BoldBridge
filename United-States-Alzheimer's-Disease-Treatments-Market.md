Application I, Symptomatic Treatments: Symptomatic treatments remain one of the most commonly utilized options in Alzheimer's care. These therapies primarily aim to manage the cognitive symptoms of Alzheimer's, such as memory loss, confusion, and disorientation. Medications like cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, and galantamine) and NMDA receptor antagonists (memantine) are often used to improve neurotransmitter activity in the brain, temporarily reducing symptoms or slowing the progression of cognitive decline. These treatments offer valuable relief to patients, although they do not halt or reverse disease progression. As the patient population ages, the demand for symptomatic treatments continues to rise. Application II, Disease-Modifying Treatments: Disease-modifying treatments represent a more recent and promising area in Alzheimer's research, aiming to slow or halt the progression of the disease rather than just addressing symptoms. The focus of these treatments is to target the underlying pathological processes of Alzheimer's, including the accumulation of amyloid plaques and tau tangles. Medications such as monoclonal antibodies (e.g., aducanumab, lecanemab) have been developed to target amyloid beta, which is thought to play a central role in the development of Alzheimer's. These treatments are expected to play a critical role in reshaping the Alzheimer's treatment landscape by potentially altering the course of the disease itself. However, the efficacy and safety of these therapies continue to be evaluated in clinical settings. Application III, Behavioral Treatments: Behavioral treatments are focused on addressing the non-cognitive symptoms of Alzheimer's, which often include aggression, anxiety, depression, and sleep disturbances. These symptoms can significantly affect the quality of life for both patients and caregivers. Behavioral interventions may involve pharmacological treatments, such as antidepressants, antipsychotics, and mood stabilizers, as well as non-pharmacological approaches like cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and environmental modifications. By targeting the behavioral aspects of Alzheimer's, these treatments aim to reduce distressing symptoms and improve the daily functioning and wellbeing of individuals. They are essential in managing the holistic needs of Alzheimer's patients, as behavioral symptoms can be just as debilitating as cognitive decline.Application IV, Supportive Therapies: Supportive therapies in Alzheimer's care are focused on providing overall care and assistance to both patients and their families. This category includes a variety of services, such as home care, physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy. These therapies are designed to enhance the patient's quality of life by maintaining or improving their functional abilities, mobility, and communication skills. Furthermore, supportive therapies include caregiver support programs and education, which are critical as caregivers play a significant role in managing the disease at home.
